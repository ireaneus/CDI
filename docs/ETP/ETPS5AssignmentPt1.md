**The Four “Ps”**

The 4 “P”s of Communicating God’s Word.

- Anyone who hears enough sermons soon gets the idea that certain preachers like alliteration. I’m not necessarily a big fan of alliteration, but there are times when alliteration is helpful in remembering the key points of a concept. When it comes to communicating God’s Word in any format or setting, whether it be evangelism, teaching, or preaching, these 4 “P”s are indispensable.

**Prayer.** Prayer is not just an addition to what we do in communicating God’s Word, it is the principle element among many that should mark us in attempting to be used by the Lord for another’s benefit. Christ modeled this essential dynamic in His public ministry. Other effective spokesmen for God have been mighty in prayer. More than any other characteristic, prayer marks out God’s long line of proclaimers in the Old Testament, New Testament, and in church history to the present day. Anyone who would follow the biblical model must take prayer very seriously. Before we ever open our mouths to communicate God’s Word, we must steep ourselves in prayer.

**Purpose.** What is our intent, our aim, our purpose in preaching or teaching the message before us? Have we thought out carefully and clearly just why we are standing before someone or a group and attempting to communicate God’s Word? Is it to educate, to entertain, to convict, to exhort, to transform, to win friends, or to see people turn to Christ? It is very easy for selfish motives and unloving motives to get mixed into what, on the surface, should be simply done to honor God and bless His people. Further, an understanding of our purpose will give us the passion for preaching that we need. If the preacher/teacher is not purposeful and excited about what he is declaring, why should the listener be?

**Preparation.** Here is where we will focus on the nuts and bolts of the lesson, sermon, or evangelistic message. Initially there is a steep learning curve here, but practice and diligence will pay off. There is no place for laziness or slipshod work when it comes to declaring God’s Word. We must *be diligent to present ourselves approved to God as a workman who does not need to be ashamed, handling accurately the word of truth* (**2 Timothy 2:15**).

**Presentation.** At some point, after much prayer, thought, and preparation we will have to stand up and open our mouths to say whatever it is the Lord is prompting us to speak. Some look ahead to the actual speaking for the Lord with dread and apprehension. We’ve all heard about the *fear of public speaking.* For some, that fear is very real and can be paralyzing. It’s also true that some people are naturally more gifted public speakers than others. But effective public speaking can be learned by anyone. Whatever fears we may have can be faced and given to the Lord for His glory. Here, again, just as with preparation, there is no substitute for practice and a willingness to learn and grow in this area.

**Exegesis and Lesson Planning**

- Now we turn to exegesis and lesson planning.
- First, there are some important words to define.

**Exegesis:** *Exegesis is the critical explanation and interpretation of a passage of Scripture.*

**Exposition:** *Exposition is the content of the sermon which exposes what the Bible says in its context to the modern audience.*

- While these two terms may appear similar on the surface, they are two necessary steps in preparation for communicating God’s Word. Exegesis, as we’ll see, is the diligent study of the text to determine just what the text says and what it means. In exposition, we take the results of our exegesis and craft a sermon around them. Perhaps most importantly, exposition adds the essential element of relevant application to our exegesis.
- So, let’s begin with choosing a passage of Scriptural text. How do we go about selecting a passage of the Bible for teaching or preaching purposes?
- The passage chosen for lesson planning is determined by several factors. If our intent is to address a topic of interest, then we would choose a text that opens that subject. Our choice of a passage of Scripture for teaching may be determined for us if we are working our way through a book of the Bible.
- In that case, our passage for teaching is the next chapter, paragraph, or unit of study in the book. Sometimes our passage will be connected to the season of the year, as for example a lesson at Christmas time or one for Resurrection Sunday.

**Original audience.** To be faithful to the Scriptures we need to keep in mind the original audience to whom the passage was addressed.
    - Who were they?
    - What situations were they facing?
    - How did this passage speak to their concerns or problems?

**Context.** In doing exegesis of any biblical text context is all important. Where does this passage fit in the author’s flow of argument? What preceded this section?

- What follows next?
- The key questions we are attempting to answer when studying any passage from Scripture are: What does the text say and what did this text mean to the original recipients? How did they understand these words from the original author? (Use **Rom. 12:1** as an example)

    - **Therefore** *What is it there for? The previous 11 chapters*

**Unit of study.** A moment ago, I referred to a unit of study, also called a unit of meaning.

- To determine the units of meaning or study in a particular book it is necessary to first have an outline of the entire book.
- That outline should show us the author’s flow of thought from one topic or subject to the next.

- Sometimes the units of study will nicely coincide with the chapter breaks. Sometimes the units of study will be determined by the paragraph breaks. And occasionally the units of study will be determined by the actual units of thought that may vary even within the paragraph itself.
- One reason that discovering the unit of study is important is that whatever size text we select for teaching; we need to be diligent in our treatment of every part of that text.

**Time.** When it comes to teaching a lesson from the Bible, time will almost always be a consideration. Whether you have a ½ hour or 1½ hours, the unit of study that you choose will have to fit your time constraints.

- In addition, time is also an issue depending on your student audience. Younger students do better with shorter lessons, while an adult class can remain attentive for longer periods of time.

**Reading the Passage.**

- At this point let’s assume you have chosen your text for study and lesson planning.
- How do we proceed from here? As noted earlier, prayer is essential throughout this process. Having sought the Lord’s help, begin by reading the text you’ve selected.
- Read it multiple times. Read the same text from different versions and translations of the Bible. As you read through the selected text notice the key words that the author chooses to use.
- Notice also words that are repeated or those words that appear to be especially important in the flow of the author’s message.

**Structural Analysis**

- Do a structural analysis of the main sentences in the passage. A structural analysis is like diagramming a sentence. Pick out the subject (with modifiers), the verb (with modifiers), and the object (with modifiers).
- (Use **Romans 12:1** as an example. The apostle Paul tended to use run-on sentences. That means he often includes extended modifiers in his statements. It will take some practice to get proficient at this step. Try this exercise again using **Rom. 15:1**.)

> "Therefore **I**(Subject, Pronoun) **urge**(Main Verb) **you**(Object), brethren, by the mercies of God, **to present**(modifying main verb) your bodies a living and holy sacrifice, acceptable to God, which is your spiritual service of worship." (Modifiers of the main verb or object)
>
> Now **we**(Subject, Pronount) who are strong **ought**(Main Verb) **to bear**(Modifier main verb) the weaknesses of **those**(Object) without strength and not just **please**(modifier verb) ourselves.

**Commentaries**

- The next step is to check some commentaries for more in-depth analysis of your text. Commentaries generally fall into two broad categories: critical and devotional.
- **Critical commentaries** explore the meaning of the text, the meanings of specific words in the text, the relevant grammar of the passage, and pertinent background and literary issues. Critical commentaries also discuss the biblical theology of your target text. Biblical theology speaks to both the author’s and the hearer’s understanding of God. It also looks at important theological developments in the passage.
- **Devotional commentaries** are usually less substantial and tend to provide insight into the emotions and sensitivities of both the author and his audience.
- The writer of a devotional commentary sometimes puts us in the place of the biblical writer’s audience so that we catch a glimpse of how they might have received what they were being told.
- This can be quite helpful in understanding the unique historical setting of the text.

**Take Notes**

- Having covered all the introductory material, it should be obvious that you could save preparation time by taking notes throughout the process just discussed.
- So, if you are called upon to present a Sunday School lesson next week, be sure you have your notebook in hand as you begin to work through the different steps just outlined.
- You’ll want to have notes on the background of the book where your text is located.
- As you work with the critical commentaries, you’ll want notes about specific word meanings, significant grammar issues in the text, and any cross references or devotional insights that you’ve come across.
- Develop your own system for taking and saving notes. They are certain to come in handy at another time.

## Background Notes — Why Paul Wrote 1 Corinthians

### Historical Setting

**City of Corinth**

- Major Greek city located on the **Isthmus of Corinth**, connecting mainland Greece with the Peloponnese.
- Controlled two important ports:

    - **Lechaeum** (west – toward Italy)
    - **Cenchreae** (east – toward Asia Minor)
- Because of this location, Corinth became a **major trade and travel hub** in the Roman Empire.
- The city was destroyed by Rome in **146 BC** and later rebuilt as a Roman colony by **Julius Caesar in 44 BC**.
- By the time Paul visited, it was a **wealthy and influential Roman city**.

### Culture of Corinth

Corinth was known for **wealth, commerce, and moral corruption**.  
The city was **cosmopolitan**, with people from:

- Greece
- Rome
- Asia Minor
- Egypt
- the Near East

It was famous for **luxury, entertainment, and immorality**.  
The phrase **“to Corinthianize”** in ancient Greek culture meant **to live in sexual immorality**.

### Religion in Corinth

Corinth was filled with **pagan temples and idol worship**.  
The most famous temple was dedicated to **Aphrodite**, the goddess of love.  
Ancient writers mention **temple prostitution** associated with worship practices.  
The city also contained:

- Roman imperial worship
- Greek philosophical influence
- Mystery religions

New Christians in Corinth were coming out of a **deeply pagan environment**.

### Paul’s Relationship with Corinth

Paul first came to Corinth on his **second missionary journey** around **AD 50–52** (**Acts 18:1–11**).  
He stayed about **18 months**, preaching the gospel and planting the church.  
Key people involved:

- **Aquila and Priscilla**
- **Crispus**, synagogue leader
- **Titius Justus**

Many Gentiles were converted.

### Time of Writing

**1 Corinthians was written around AD 52–56**.
Paul likely wrote the letter from **Ephesus** during his **third missionary journey** (**1 Cor. 16:8**).

### Why Paul Wrote the Letter

Paul wrote because the Corinthian church had **serious problems**.

> Remember, none of the four gospels have been written and distributed by this time.  
> Virtually none of the letters of the New Testament have been authored  
> The church has the Old Testament and the teaching of Paul and its other leaders  
> So in the absence of such grounding and discipleship, the church was highly susceptible to wrong teaching and unhealthy practices  
> What kind of bizarre practices would invade our church were it not for the word of God?  
> In fact, we do see exactly the same tendency in the church today  
> Where the word of God and the doctrines of the church are not taught, the practices of the faithful quickly begin to deviate and become corrupted

In *AD 54* a letter is written by Paul to warn about vice and immoral conduct (1 Cor. 5:9).  But that letter no longer exists. Later Paul hears of factions developing in the church (7:1) so in *AD 56*, he writes his second letter, which we hold as 1 Corinthians.

> Finally, Paul later made a *“painful visit”* (as he called it) to Corinth in *AD 58*, probably because his letter hadnʼt succeeded in correcting their sin
> It was during his second stay in the city that Paul sat down to write Romans
> he came to understand how essential good doctrine is to the conduct of godly church life
> That is a truth that has been set aside in many churches again today

That is the central theme of the letter: *how to live out a godly (spiritual) life*

If Romans can be considered the preeminent letter on Christian theology  
Then 1 Corinthians is the New Testamentʼs principle letter for the pastoral application of theology in everyday church life

- This is a letter that demonstrates how important theology is to the proper conduct of Christian life, particularly the life of the gathering

The letter reads with two discrete sections:  
First, Chapters 1-6 addresses all Paul has heard about the goings on in the
church

- The factions
- The false human wisdom
- The carnal conditions
- The lack of order and discipline and respect for the Apostlesʼ authority

The second section in Chapters 7-16 is where Paul answers the questions posed

- This explains why the letter reads a little like a laundry list of issues less doctrine, more practice

Therefore, this letter is utterly contemporary and highly applicable to our day. The same issues Paul addressed in Corinth continue to weigh on the church today.

- The celebrity status of church leaders and the factions that develop around them?
- The manner and source of our salvation?
- Divorce and remarriage?

- The integration of the pagan practices in the church?

## Commentaries — 1 Corinthians 4:1–5

### Verse By Verse Ministries International

*(Pastor Stephen Armstrong — 1 Cor. 3:16–23; 4:1–13)*

Paul continues addressing the **divisions within the Corinthian church** that began earlier in the letter where believers were claiming allegiance to particular teachers (e.g., *“I am of Paul,” “I am of Apollos”* — **1 Cor. 1:12**).

The Corinthians had begun **elevating certain leaders**, which produced pride and factionalism within the church.

#### Context from 1 Cor. 3

Paul reminds the church that **human leaders are not the source of spiritual growth**.

- The church is **God’s building** (**1 Cor. 3:9**).
- Spiritual growth occurs **only according to God’s work and decision** (**1 Cor. 3:6–7**).

Because of this truth, believers must reject worldly thinking.

- Paul says the believer must become **“foolish” in the eyes of the world** in order to become truly wise (**1 Cor. 3:18**).

Discipleship occurs in the **ordinary, biblical way**:

- Teaching believers to **obey all that Christ commanded** through the Word of God (**Matt. 28:20**).

Paul concludes the previous section by stating:

> *“All things belong to you”* (**1 Cor. 3:21–23**)

This means no leader or teacher within the church should be elevated in such a way that believers create **exclusive allegiances or spiritual hierarchies**.

Instead, the entire body of Christ shares the same spiritual inheritance:

- The **same origin in faith**
- The **same power of the Holy Spirit**
- The **same destiny in Christ**

All spiritual leaders ultimately serve **the entire body of Christ**, not personal followings.

### Paul’s Explanation of Leadership

**1 Corinthians 4:1–5**

Paul now explains the **proper way believers should view church leaders and apostles**.

> *“Let a man regard us in this manner, as servants of Christ and stewards of the mysteries of God.”* (**1 Cor. 4:1**)

#### Servants of Christ

The word translated **“servant”** is the Greek word *huperetēs*.

- Literally means **“under-rower”**, referring to the lowest-level rower on a ship.
- It conveys the idea of **subordinate service under authority**.

Paul’s point is that apostles and teachers are **not spiritual celebrities**, but **servants operating under Christ’s command**.

#### Stewards of the Mysteries of God

Leaders are also called **stewards**.

A steward is someone **entrusted with responsibility over another’s property**.

The *“mysteries of God”* refers to the **revealed truths of the gospel** that were once hidden but are now revealed in Christ (**Eph. 3:3–6**).

The essential requirement of a steward is **faithfulness**, not popularity or status (**1 Cor. 4:2**).

### Human Judgment vs. God’s Judgment

Paul warns the Corinthians against evaluating leaders according to **human standards**.

> *“But to me it is a very small thing that I may be examined by you, or by any human court…”* (**1 Cor. 4:3**)

Paul is not dismissing accountability, but he is rejecting **human pride and comparison** as the basis for spiritual judgment.

The Corinthians were **ranking leaders** and comparing their ministries.

This behavior reflects:

- **Worldly thinking**
- **Pride**
- **Misunderstanding of spiritual service**

Paul reminds them that **God alone is the final judge of every servant’s work**.

> *“Therefore do not go on passing judgment before the time…”* (**1 Cor. 4:5**)  

Only Christ can properly evaluate:

- Motives of the heart
- Faithfulness of service
- The true value of a person’s ministry

This connects with Paul’s earlier metaphor of building materials:

- **gold, silver, precious stones**
- **wood, hay, straw**
  (**1 Cor. 3:12–13**)

Human beings are **poor judges of spiritual value**, but Christ will reveal all things at His coming.

Therefore believers must **stop assigning spiritual status to one another**.

The church must remember that every servant ultimately **serves the Master, not the approval of people**.

### Supporting Commentary Insights

#### Gordon Fee — *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*

Fee notes that Paul is correcting the Corinthians’ **“hero worship” of leaders**.

> “The Corinthians had adopted the cultural tendency to evaluate leaders by status and rhetorical skill rather than faithfulness to Christ.”

Paul therefore reframes leadership as **service under Christ’s authority**, not as a platform for prestige.

---

#### John MacArthur — *MacArthur New Testament Commentary*

MacArthur emphasizes that the word **steward** highlights **accountability to God alone**.

> “The steward is not evaluated by the servants in the household but by the master who appointed him.”

Thus believers must avoid **premature judgments** of ministers or fellow servants.

---

#### David Garland — *BECNT: 1 Corinthians*

Garland explains that the Corinthians were applying **worldly systems of honor and ranking** to church leadership.

Paul dismantles this thinking by reminding them:

- Leaders are **servants**
- They manage **God’s message**
- **Only God determines their reward**

---

### Key Observations from the Commentaries

- Church leaders are **servants under Christ**, not personalities to follow.
- Spiritual leadership is defined by **faithfulness**, not human approval.
- The church must avoid **ranking believers or comparing ministries**.
- Only Christ will fully reveal the value of each servant’s work at His return.

---

### Application

The Corinthian problem is still common today.

Believers often elevate certain teachers, ministries, or personalities.

Paul’s correction reminds the church that:

- All leaders are **servants of Christ**.
- The gospel message belongs to **God**, not the messenger.
- Final evaluation belongs **only to the Lord**.

Therefore the church must focus on **faithful service rather than human comparison or status**.

Later in **1 Cor. 4:6**, Paul explains that he used **himself and Apollos as examples** so the Corinthians would learn **not to exalt one leader over another**.

This shows that Paul’s teaching about **“us” (the apostles)** was meant to **correct the behavior of the Corinthian church**.

## Word Study

### Servants

**Greek:** *huperetēs* (ὑπηρέτης)

**Meaning:**
A subordinate servant or assistant. The word originally referred to an **“under-rower” on a ship**, someone who rowed under the direction of a superior.

**Significance in the passage:**
Paul uses this word to describe the apostles. Their role is not one of prestige or status but **humble service under Christ’s authority**. The apostles serve **Christ first**, not the opinions of men.

### Stewards

## Summary — 1 Corinthians 4:1–5

In this passage Paul continues addressing the divisions within the Corinthian church where believers were aligning themselves with different leaders such as Paul and Apollos. Paul explains that church leaders should not be viewed as personalities to follow but rather as servants of Christ and stewards entrusted with the mysteries of God (1 Cor. 4:1). The primary requirement of a steward is faithfulness, not popularity or approval from people (1 Cor. 4:2). Therefore believers should stop passing judgment on one another’s ministries or assigning status to different leaders, because only Christ will reveal the true motives and value of each person’s work when He returns (1 Cor. 4:5). The passage teaches that all servants in the church are accountable to Christ, and final evaluation belongs to Him alone.
